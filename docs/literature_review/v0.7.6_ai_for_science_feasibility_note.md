# v0.7.6 AI-for-Science Feasibility Note

## Purpose

Document the feasibility of AI-for-Science methods as future exploratory extensions without authorizing implementation.

This note provides high-level conceptual context only. It does not constitute project evidence, validation, or endorsement of any specific method.

---

## 1. AI-for-Science as Research Direction

AI-for-Science is a broad research direction applying machine learning to scientific modeling, simulation, and discovery. Notable examples in materials science and physics include structure prediction, molecular dynamics acceleration, and PDE solving.

*External context; not used as project evidence.*

## 2. PINNs as Possible PDE Approximation Method

Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) approximate solutions to PDEs by training neural networks to minimize PDE residuals, boundary condition violations, and optionally data mismatch. They have been applied to various PDEs including Poisson, diffusion, and Navier-Stokes equations in the literature.

**Relevance to this project:** The project solves thermal diffusion, defect reaction-diffusion, and (future) Poisson equations. PINNs could theoretically approximate these, but:

- The project's structured grids and well-posed elliptic problems are efficiently handled by classical finite-difference methods.
- PINNs introduce stochastic training, hyperparameter sensitivity, and reduced interpretability.
- No classical solver baseline exists yet for C3 coupling against which to benchmark.

**Assessment:** Potentially feasible for future parametric studies or inverse problems, but not a priority and not justified before classical baselines exist. No claim is made that PINNs would be faster, more accurate, or more suitable than the classical solver for this project's equations.

## 3. Surrogate Models as Possible Emulator

Surrogate models are trained on classical solver outputs to provide fast approximate predictions for parameter sweeps, sensitivity analysis, or optimization.

**Relevance to this project:** After a classical solver produces validated outputs for a range of parameters, a surrogate could accelerate parameter exploration.

**Assessment:** Requires classical solver outputs first. Not feasible until C3 coupling produces baseline data. No claim is made that surrogates would provide physical validation.

## 4. Operator Learning as Future Possibility

Operator learning methods (DeepONet, Fourier Neural Operator) learn mappings between function spaces, potentially generalizing across different source terms or boundary conditions.

**Relevance to this project:** Could theoretically map defect distributions to electrostatic potentials after sufficient classical data generation.

**Assessment:** Highly speculative for this project. Requires substantial classical data generation infrastructure. Not a near-term consideration.

## 5. Why Classical Baselines Remain Mandatory

- The classical solver is interpretable, traceable, and dimensionally consistent.
- Physical behavior can be verified term-by-term.
- Convergence analysis is well-understood.
- No ML method can validate itself; external baselines are required.
- The project's governance framework (ADR, TDD, quality gates) is designed for deterministic numerical methods.

## 6. Why No ML Implementation Now

- C3 classical solver coupling is not yet implemented.
- No physical phi has been resolved.
- No benchmark baseline exists.
- No dependency decision has been made.
- No RED tests exist for AI track.
- Risk of overclaim is high.
- Resources should focus on C3 classical roadmap.

## 7. Why No Dependency Now

- ML frameworks add significant install time and disk usage.
- Transitive dependency chains are complex and fragile.
- GPU dependencies may break CPU-only CI.
- Version conflicts with numpy/scipy are possible.
- No scientific justification exists yet.

## 8. Why No AlphaFold-Style Claim Is Allowed

AlphaFold succeeded because:
- It solved a well-defined prediction task (protein structure from sequence).
- It was trained on massive experimental databases (PDB).
- Its predictions were validated against experimental structures (CASP competition).

This project has:
- No experimental device data.
- No well-defined prediction target for ML.
- No external validation benchmark.
- No competition or independent assessment.

Therefore, any analogy to AlphaFold, GNoME, MatterGen, or similar large-scale AI-for-Science successes is classified as **high-level conceptual context, not project evidence**, and cannot be used to justify implementation decisions.

## 9. Future Evidence Requirements

Before any AI-for-Science code can be written, the following must exist:

1. Accepted ADR (not just proposed).
2. Dependency decision brief with CI impact analysis.
3. Analytic benchmark with known exact solution.
4. Classical solver baseline for comparison.
5. RED tests encoding isolation, reproducibility, and error contracts.
6. Uncertainty quantification methodology.
7. Human decision log entry approving activation.
8. Rollback plan.
9. Separation architecture enforced.
10. No physical overclaim in any output or documentation.
